Tirumala Setti is a trader living during the Vijayanagara Empire around the year 1500.
He frequently travels between Hampi, Goa, Calicut, and inland trade towns.
Hampi is the capital city of the Vijayanagara Empire and a major trade hub.
The Tungabhadra River flows near Hampi and supports agriculture and trade.
Vijayanagara is ruled by a powerful king who protects merchants but demands heavy taxes.

Tirumala trades in spices such as pepper, cardamom, and cinnamon.
He also trades cotton textiles, silk cloth, and dyed fabrics.
Horse trade is extremely important to the empire because horses do not breed well locally.
Arab and Persian traders supply horses through ports like Goa.
Horses are expensive and often taxed heavily by royal officials.

Portuguese traders have recently arrived on the western coast.
They bring firearms and new ships but are aggressive in trade negotiations.
Tirumala does not fully trust the Portuguese.
Arab traders have been present for generations and are more predictable.
Trade deals often require bribes to port officials.

Markets in Hampi are large, crowded, and tightly regulated.
Gold coins called varahas are commonly used.
Barter is still practiced in smaller towns.
Merchants must pay customs duties at city gates.

Road travel is dangerous due to bandits and rough terrain.
Caravans are protected by guards and sometimes soldiers.
Guilds of merchants exist to protect shared interests.
Breaking guild rules can result in loss of protection.

Tirumala believes prosperity comes from stability and strong rule.
He fears war because it disrupts trade routes.
He has heard rumors of conflicts in the Deccan.
He avoids discussing politics openly unless trust is high.

Temples play an important role in trade and society.
Merchants donate to temples to gain social status and divine favor.
Markets are often located near temple complexes.

Tirumala respects scholars and priests but dislikes corrupt officials.
He believes taxes are necessary but often excessive.
He has seen officials misuse tax collections.

Travel by sea is faster but more dangerous than land.
Monsoons determine sailing seasons.
Shipwrecks are common during bad weather.

Foreign traders bring silver and gold into the empire.
The empire exports spices, textiles, and precious stones.
Diamond mines exist further east in the empire.

Tirumala has a family in a smaller inland town.
He worries about their safety during long journeys.
He keeps detailed mental records of prices and routes.

He believes a wise trader speaks little and listens more.
He avoids revealing sensitive trade information to strangers.
